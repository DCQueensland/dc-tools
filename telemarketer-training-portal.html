Test Your Knowledge
15 questions across all call types and techniques
Q1. What's the very first thing you should do after introducing yourself on a new relationship call?
A) Pitch the Dale Carnegie programs
B) Ask "Did I catch you at a bad time?"
C) Ask about their training budget
D) Send them an email
Always ask permission first: "Did I catch you at a bad time?" This shows respect and gives them the choice to continue. If they say no, you've got their attention.
Q2. What are the 3 main pressure areas leaders face that you should mention?
A) Sales targets, marketing, technology
B) Holding onto best people, building teams that own outcomes, achieving results in an unpredictable market
C) Hiring, training, compliance
D) Revenue, costs, cash flow
The 3 pressure points: (1) Holding onto best people, (2) Building teams that get results/own outcomes, (3) Achieving results in an unpredictable market. Then ask "How does that compare to what you're seeing?"
Q3. How long is the meeting you're booking with William?
A) 15 minutes
B) 30 minutes
C) 1 hour
D) However long they need
"It's just a quick 15-minute Teams chat." Keep it short and low-commitment — this removes barriers to saying yes.
Q4. When someone says "We're too busy right now," what technique do you use?
A) Ask them to call back when they're free
B) Feel, Felt, Found
C) Offer a discount
D) Send an email instead
Feel, Felt, Found: "I completely understand (Feel). Most leaders I speak with are flat out. Others have felt the same way (Felt). What they found was that 15 minutes with William often gave them back hours (Found)."
Q5. After booking the appointment, what should you ask them to do while still on the phone?
A) Share with their team
B) Check their calendar and accept the invite
C) Visit the website
D) Nothing — just hang up
"Could you check your calendar and accept it while we're on the line? That way it's locked in." This dramatically reduces no-shows.
Q6. When calling a workshop attendee who is NOT a decision maker, what's the key qualifying question?
A) "What's your budget?"
B) "Does your company invest in their people, or is professional development something you'd invest in yourself?"
C) "Can you put me through to your manager?"
D) "Are you interested in a course?"
You need to figure out if the company pays or they pay themselves. This determines whether you're booking a coaching call or moving on. If your gut says it's a waste of time, ask about future workshops and get off the phone — NEXT!
Q7. What's the "magic wand" question used for?
A) To close the sale
B) To qualify their pain and find what they'd fix
C) To build rapport
D) To establish credibility
"If I had a magic wand and could improve one or two things about how your people develop skills — whether it's leadership presence, sales effectiveness, or team culture — would there be anything to fix?" This uncovers their real pain.
Q8. How many years of history does Dale Carnegie have?
A) 50 years
B) 80 years
C) 113 years
D) 150 years
113-year history. World's largest privately-owned training company. Partners with 428 of the Fortune 500. Know these numbers — they build instant credibility.
Q9. When a prospect says "Send me some info first," what should you do?
A) Send a brochure and wait
B) Tell them there's no info to send
C) Feel, Felt, Found — a live chat is far more valuable because William tailors it to their situation
D) Email the website link
"I get that. Many leaders felt the same way. What they found was that a live chat was far more valuable, because William could tailor it to their exact situation. How about a quick 15 minutes Thursday or Friday, then I can send tailored material after?"
Q10. What's the "upfront contract" before booking a meeting?
A) Getting them to sign a commitment form
B) Sending terms and conditions
C) "If you're 10 minutes in and it's not relevant — just let us know, and we'll do the same. Sound fair?"
D) Asking for payment details
The upfront contract removes pressure and builds trust. It shows confidence in the value of the meeting and gives them an easy out — which paradoxically makes them more likely to attend.
Q11. When calling a workshop registrant who DIDN'T attend, what should you ask first?
A) "Would you like to rebook?"
B) "Briefly, can I ask why you registered for the workshop in the first place?"
C) "Can I speak to your manager?"
D) "Would you like us to send the recording?"
Find out WHY they registered — that tells you their pain point and interest level. Then use that to book the coaching call: "During the workshop we offered a coaching call valued at $200..."
Q12. Which Queensland companies should you name-drop for credibility?
A) Woolworths, Coles, Telstra
B) Hutchinson Builders, Bradnam's Windows and Doors, IOR, BDO
C) Google, Microsoft, Apple
D) NAB, ANZ, Westpac
Local Queensland companies they'll recognise: Hutchinson Builders, Bradnam's Windows and Doors, IOR, and BDO. These are "facing similar challenges to YOU!" — it's relatable credibility.
Q13. What's the value of the coaching call offered to workshop attendees?
A) Free
B) $100
C) $200
D) $500
"During the workshop we offered a coaching call valued at $200 for the 15 minutes." Framing it as a $200 value makes the call feel like a gift, not a sales pitch.
Q14. How should you close with a day/time option?
A) "When are you free?"
B) "I'll email you some options"
C) "Would Thursday or Friday suit you better? Morning or afternoon?"
D) "Let me know when works"
Always give a choice between two options, not an open question. "Thursday or Friday?" then "Morning or afternoon?" then lock in a specific time. This moves them to action.
Q15. If your gut tells you a non-DM workshop attendee is a waste of time, what should you do?
A) Keep pushing for a booking
B) Ask to speak to their manager
C) Ask if they'd like to be notified for future workshops, then get off the phone — NEXT!
D) Send them a brochure
Don't waste time on dead ends. Ask about future workshops to keep it friendly, then move on. Your time is better spent on the next call. NEXT!

Subject Line
The 5 Barriers to Productivity Every Business Faces
The 5 Barriers
1. Engagement — when staff feel overlooked, energy and loyalty vanish
2. Communication — mixed messages and unclear priorities drain time
3. Collaboration — silos stop teams from sharing ideas
4. Adaptability — change is constant; resistance slows everything
5. Accountability — without ownership, goals slip and frustration builds

First 15 seconds — respect, permission, and a human connection

Expert Video: The Opening

Super Script Version
"Hi [First Name], this is [Your Name] from Dale Carnegie Australia. I'm ringing on behalf of our Managing Director William Farmer. Did I catch you at a bad time?"

(If no)
"Thanks — I'll keep this brief. Could I take 30 seconds to explain why I'm calling, and you can decide if it's worth continuing?"
Consulting Version (More Casual)
"[First Name], it's Mike calling from Dale Carnegie. How's things?"

If they say "Good, how are you?"
"Busy, you know how it is. I'm sure it's the same in your world."

If they sound rude/short
"I didn't catch you at a bad time, did I?"
(Match their tone — mirror without losing courtesy)
Use their first name with curiosity and energy. Smile when you speak — they can hear it.

4. Customer's World — The 3 Pressure Points
Empathy first — connect with their reality before pitching
▼
▶

Expert Video: The Customer's World

Place video in /videos/tm-04-pressure-points.mp4

Say This
"A lot of leaders I'm speaking with right now are telling me they're under pressure in three main areas:
Holding onto their best people,
Building teams that own outcomes,
Achieving results in an unpredictable market.

How does that compare to what you're seeing in your business?"
Listen. Acknowledge. Mirror back. Don't rush past their answer — this is where you learn what matters to them.
Generic Alternative (use 2 or 3)
"The major concerns many of our clients face are:
Attracting and maintaining key staff for their business to thrive
Engaging staff to take ownership to improve business performance
Innovating resources to achieve budget in a challenging market
Keeping a culture of productivity within internal and remote teams

I guess you'd encounter those issues from time to time?"

5. Qualifying Questions
Learn the lay of the land — do they invest in development? What's working? What's not?
▼
▶

Expert Video: Qualifying Questions

Place video in /videos/tm-05-qualifying.mp4

Ask These (Consulting Approach)
"How does your business currently invest in professional development?"
"What type of training or coaching do you already run for your people?"
"Why those programs or approaches?"
"How effective have they been in practice? Are people applying it, or is it more a tick-the-box exercise?"
"Who usually drives these decisions — is it HR, L&D, or line managers?"
Positioning Statement
"Usually when I speak with businesses, the struggles aren't about whether training happens — it's that leaders don't see real behavioural change, teams still lack confidence in client conversations, or engagement drops once the course ends. Are you seeing any of that, or am I completely off base?"
The Magic Wand Question
"If I had a magic wand and could improve one or two things about how your people develop skills — whether it's leadership presence, sales effectiveness, or team culture — would there be anything to fix?"

Lock It In
"Great. I'll send that invite through right now — could you check your calendar and accept it while we're on the line? That way it's locked in."
Optional Quick Discovery (if rapport is strong)
"Before we finish, can I ask you one quick question to help William prepare?"
"What's working really well for your team right now?"
"Where do you see the biggest pressure point ahead?"
"That'll help make the 15 minutes as valuable as possible."

"The only thing we ask: if you're 10 minutes into our meeting and you realise the conversation isn't relevant to what we discussed today — just let us know, and we'll do the same. Sound fair?"
Close the Call
"Thanks, [First Name]. Looking forward to connecting with you on [day/time]. Have a fantastic rest of your week!"
William has 30 years of experience and your meeting will be insightful as well as impactful. End on energy!

Short Voicemail (Recommended)
"[Name], it's [your name]. Give me a call back."
(That's it. Short, curious, they'll call back.)
Long Voicemail
"Hey [Name], it's [your name] giving you a call from Dale Carnegie Australia. Have been working with tons of [their industry] lately. Appreciate if you can give me a quick call back."
Short is usually better. Curiosity gets callbacks. Long pitches on voicemail get deleted.

The Feel — Felt — Found Framework

Feel: "I completely understand..." (acknowledge their concern)
Felt: "Others have felt the same way..." (normalise it)
Found: "What they found was..." (give evidence of the opposite)
⏰ "We're too busy right now"
▼
Response
"I completely understand — most leaders I speak with are flat out. Others have felt the same way. What they found was that 15 minutes with William often gave them back hours, because they walked away with simple, practical ideas. Would Thursday morning or Friday afternoon be easier?"
📚 "We already have training in place"
▼
Response
"That makes sense — most good companies do. Some of our clients felt the same, including Hutchinson Builders. What they found was that adding Carnegie's approach actually amplified what they already had. Would you be open to exploring how that might work in a short call?"
📩 "Send me some info first"
▼
Response
"I get that. Many leaders felt the same way. What they found was that a live chat was far more valuable, because William could tailor it to their exact situation. How about a quick 15 minutes Thursday or Friday, then I can send tailored material after?"
💰 "We don't have budget right now"
▼
Response
"I understand — many are tightening up. Clients like Woolworths felt the same. What they found was that even small shifts in engagement created measurable savings — one store manager saved $600,000 from a single project. That's why William keeps it short and practical — would you be open to Friday?"
🎓 "I did the Dale Carnegie Course years ago"
▼
Response
"We really enjoy catching up with graduates! William would love to invest the time with you to share how we support our graduates and keep them up to date with best practices. There's a lot of change at the moment, and keeping people productive is so important. With that in mind, how is your schedule on Thursday or Friday?"
⏳ "It's just not the right time"
▼
Response
"Timing is everything, and with a quick investment of 10-15 minutes, you'll have an outline of some of the best strategies that can help your business this year. With that in mind, how is your schedule on Thursday or Friday?"
❓ "What will be covered in the meeting?"
▼
Response
"The agenda is:
Find out more about what you're wanting to achieve as a leader
Some of the challenges you might be facing
How to improve the performance of your business
How to get stronger returns and performance from your people

How's your schedule Thursday or Friday? I have an opening at..."
